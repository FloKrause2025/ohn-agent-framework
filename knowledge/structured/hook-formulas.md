## What This KB Is For

This KB gives Scripty 56 proven hook formula patterns for writing the first 3–5 seconds of a scam-awareness video script. Each formula is a reusable pattern — not a script to copy verbatim, but a structural template to adapt to the specific scam topic.

## Why the Agent Needs This

Without formula patterns, Scripty invents hooks from scratch every time. This produces inconsistent quality — some hooks land, most don't. The formulas in this KB are drawn from OHN's highest-performing videos and proven short-form content patterns. They encode what works so Scripty doesn't have to rediscover it each time.

> **If ignored:** Hook quality becomes unpredictable. Weak hooks kill videos before the content even starts. A 1% drop in 3-second retention can cost hundreds of thousands of views.

---

## The Psychology of Hooks

Every hook works by creating a **Knowledge Gap** — a question the viewer's brain needs answered before it can move on. The viewer stops scrolling because their brain is uncomfortable leaving a question unanswered.

There are 7 psychological triggers that reliably open a Knowledge Gap:

| Trigger | What it does | Best formula categories |
|---|---|---|
| **Betrayal of trust** | Makes a familiar thing feel threatening | Revelation, OHN Signature |
| **Fear of loss** | Threatens something the viewer values | Warning/Urgency, Personal |
| **Curiosity gap** | Raises a question the viewer needs answered | Curiosity/Question |
| **Identity relevance** | Makes the viewer feel directly addressed | Personal/Relatable |
| **Assumption challenge** | Contradicts something the viewer believes to be true | Contrast/Myth-Busting |
| **Insider access** | Offers knowledge most people don't have | Insider Knowledge |
| **Urgency** | Creates pressure to act or watch immediately | Warning/Urgency |

> **Why this matters:** Scripty should choose formulas based on which psychological trigger best fits the scam topic — not just which formula sounds good. A scam about trusted contacts calls for "Betrayal of trust." A scam about everyday behaviour calls for "Assumption challenge."

## The 3 C's — Non-Negotiable Check

Every hook — regardless of formula used — must pass all three before it is submitted:

**CLARITY** — Does a viewer who knows nothing about this scam understand exactly what it is within 5 seconds?
> ❌ "A new Gmail scam" — what about Gmail? What kind of scam?
> ✅ "Scammers can use your Gmail address to open fake accounts — just by adding a dot"

**CONTEXT** — Is the hook grounded in something familiar (a real app, a real action, a real feeling)?
> ❌ "A message arrives" — what message? From who?
> ✅ "A text from your bank arrives — but it is not from your bank"

**CURIOSITY** — Does the hook raise a question the viewer must watch to answer?
> ❌ "Here is how to stay safe from phishing scams" — tells me what I'll get, creates no gap
> ✅ "Most people confirm this email without knowing what they just did" — what did they do?

> **If any one of the three C's is missing — rewrite. Do not submit a hook that fails this check.**

## How Scripty Uses This KB

**Step 1:** Read the scam topic and Googly's research report.

**Step 2:** Identify which psychological trigger best fits the scam:
- Does it exploit a trusted tool? → Revelation or OHN Signature
- Is it spreading rapidly? → Warning/Urgency
- Does it challenge a safety assumption? → Contrast/Myth-Busting
- Is it highly personal/family-relevant? → Personal/Relatable

**Step 3:** Select 5–6 candidate formulas from the matching category.

**Step 4:** Adapt each template to the specific scam topic. Never copy verbatim.

**Step 5:** Write 3 hook variations from the strongest formulas.

**Step 6:** Apply the 3 C's check to all 3. Eliminate any that fail.

**Step 7:** Submit the 3 strongest hooks as options.

> **Rule:** Always draw from at least 2 different categories. A Revelation hook and a Personal hook covering the same scam gives the owner genuine choice of angle.

### CATEGORY 1: REVELATION HOOKS
*Best for: Scams that exploit something familiar. The viewer discovers their trusted tool has been weaponised.*
*Psychology trigger: Betrayal of trust*

---

**REV-001 — Familiar Betrayal**
> Template: `"Scammers have found a way to [shocking_action] using your [familiar_thing]."`
> Example: `"Scammers have found a way to open fake PayPal accounts using your Gmail address."`
> When to use: When the scam turns a trusted everyday tool against the user.
> Psychology: Betrayal of a trusted object. The familiar becomes dangerous.
> ✅ Proven: Based on the Gmail Dot Scam — OHN's highest share-rate video (8.9%).

**REV-002 — Trending Alert**
> Template: `"There is a scam targeting [specific_group] right now. Here is what to know."`
> Example: `"There is a scam targeting WhatsApp users right now. Here is what to know."`
> When to use: When Researchy flags HIGH urgency (3+ Reddit posts, recent).
> Psychology: Urgency + identity relevance. "Right now" creates immediacy.

**REV-003 — App-Specific Warning**
> Template: `"If you use [common_app_or_service], you need to see this."`
> Example: `"If you use Apple Pay, you need to see this."`
> When to use: When the scam targets users of one specific, widely-used platform.
> Psychology: Identity filter. Only people who use the app feel addressed — but that is almost everyone.

**REV-004 — Belief Shatter**
> Template: `"Everything you thought you knew about [common_safety_belief] is wrong."`
> Example: `"Everything you thought you knew about spotting a fake email is wrong."`
> When to use: When the scam bypasses a well-known protection method.
> Psychology: Challenges existing belief. Forces the viewer to question their current safety behaviour.

**REV-005 — Hidden Danger**
> Template: `"Most people do not know this scam exists. Here is how it works."`
> Example: `"Most people do not know SIM swapping exists. Here is how it works."`
> When to use: When the scam type is genuinely obscure or underreported.
> Psychology: Insider access + social proof. "Most people don't know" implies the viewer is about to get exclusive knowledge.

**REV-006 — Perspective Shift**
> Template: `"You will look at [familiar_thing] differently after the next 30 seconds."`
> Example: `"You will look at your phone number differently after the next 30 seconds."`
> When to use: When the scam reframes something the viewer uses daily without thinking about it.
> Psychology: Curiosity gap. Promises a change in how the viewer sees the world.

**REV-007 — Suppressed Truth**
> Template: `"Why is nobody talking about [scam_type]?"`
> Example: `"Why is nobody talking about AI voice cloning scams?"`
> When to use: When the scam is genuinely underreported relative to how dangerous it is.
> Psychology: Insider access + social validation. Implies the viewer is getting something others are missing.

**REV-008 — Authority Reveal** *(new)*
> Template: `"There is a reason [trusted_institution] is warning people about [scam]. Here it is."`
> Example: `"There is a reason the FBI is warning people about AI voice scams. Here it is."`
> When to use: When Googly's research found a Tier 1 government source actively issuing warnings.
> Psychology: Authority transfer. Borrowed credibility from a trusted institution.

**REV-009 — Minimal Entry** *(new)*
> Template: `"Scammers only need [small_piece_of_information] to [devastating_outcome]."`
> Example: `"Scammers only need your phone number to drain your bank account."`
> When to use: When the scam mechanism requires surprisingly little information to execute.
> Psychology: Shock at asymmetry. The viewer expects big threats to require big breaches.

**REV-010 — Scale Reveal** *(new)*
> Template: `"[Large_number] people have already been targeted by this. You probably know one of them."`
> Example: `"4 million people were hit by this scam last year. You probably know one of them."`
> When to use: When Googly's research contains strong statistics on victim numbers.
> Psychology: Social proximity. Moves the threat from abstract to personal network.

### CATEGORY 2: WARNING / URGENCY HOOKS
*Best for: Scams that are actively spreading. The viewer needs to act or warn someone NOW.*
*Psychology trigger: Urgency + Fear of loss*

---

**WARN-001 — Right Now Warning**
> Template: `"There is a [scam_type] targeting [specific_group] right now — and most people have no idea."`
> Example: `"There is a fake HMRC text targeting UK adults right now — and most people have no idea."`
> When to use: When Researchy flags HIGH urgency (3+ Reddit posts, recent).
> Psychology: Urgency + Identity relevance. "Right now" removes the sense of distant threat.

**WARN-002 — Time Pressure**
> Template: `"If you have received [specific_thing] in the last [time_period] — do not ignore it."`
> Example: `"If you have received a text about an undelivered parcel in the last 48 hours — do not ignore it."`
> When to use: When the scam is tied to a specific trigger event (delivery, refund, account alert).
> Psychology: Urgency + personal relevance. Forces immediate self-check.

**WARN-003 — Spread Warning**
> Template: `"This scam is spreading. Here is what it looks like so you can stop it before it reaches your family."`
> Example: `"This scam is spreading fast. Here is what it looks like so you can stop it before it reaches your parents."`
> When to use: When the mechanism involves forwarding (e.g. WhatsApp-style, hacked account re-sends).
> Psychology: Urgency + Fear of loss. Shifts motivation from self-protection to family protection.

**WARN-004 — Don't Be Next**
> Template: `"Thousands of people have already fallen for this. Here is why — and how to make sure you are not next."`
> Example: `"Thousands of people have already lost money to this banking scam. Here is why — and how to make sure you are not next."`
> When to use: When Googly's research contains victim statistics.
> Psychology: Social proof of danger + urgency. "You are not next" makes it personal.

**WARN-005 — Alert Trigger**
> Template: `"If you get a [specific_message_or_call] this week — this is what it actually is."`
> Example: `"If you get a call from 'Amazon' about an unauthorised charge this week — this is what it actually is."`
> When to use: When the scam involves a specific, recognisable trigger message or call.
> Psychology: Pattern recognition + urgency. The viewer may have already received this.

**WARN-006 — Family Alert**
> Template: `"Before you scroll past — your parents need to know about this."`
> Example: `"Before you scroll past — your parents need to know about this PayPal scam."`
> When to use: When the scam specifically targets older adults.
> Psychology: Moral obligation. Engages the audience through family responsibility, not personal fear.

**WARN-007 — Already Targeted**
> Template: `"If this has already happened to you — here is how to limit the damage."`
> Example: `"If a scammer has already had access to your phone — here is how to limit the damage."`
> When to use: When the scam is at scale and some viewers may already be victims.
> Psychology: High relevance + urgency for people who have already been hit but don't know what to do.

**WARN-008 — Don't Click**
> Template: `"Stop. If you are about to click on [specific_thing] — watch this first."`
> Example: `"Stop. If you are about to click on a link in a bank text message — watch this first."`
> When to use: When the scam requires a single click action that triggers the fraud.
> Psychology: Pattern interrupt. Grabs attention mid-action. Forces pause before a costly mistake.

**WARN-009 — Cost of Missing It** *(new)*
> Template: `"The average victim of this scam loses [amount]. Here is how they get tricked."`
> Example: `"The average victim of this scam loses £3,000. Here is how they get tricked."`
> When to use: When Googly's research includes average financial loss data.
> Psychology: Concrete fear of loss. A real number lands harder than a vague warning.

**WARN-010 — New Variant** *(new)*
> Template: `"Scammers have updated this trick. The old version you know about is not the dangerous one anymore."`
> Example: `"Scammers have updated the phone number spoofing trick. The old version you know about is not the dangerous one anymore."`
> When to use: When the scam is an evolved or new variant of a commonly-known type.
> Psychology: Assumption challenge + urgency. Prior knowledge becomes a false sense of security.

### CATEGORY 3: CURIOSITY / QUESTION HOOKS
*Best for: Scams with a surprising or counterintuitive mechanism. The viewer needs to understand HOW.*
*Psychology trigger: Curiosity gap*

---

**CUR-001 — The How**
> Template: `"How are scammers using [familiar_thing] to [shocking_outcome]?"`
> Example: `"How are scammers using your voicemail to steal your bank details?"`
> When to use: When the scam mechanism is genuinely surprising and non-obvious.
> Psychology: Direct curiosity gap. The question is the hook — the viewer cannot move on without the answer.

**CUR-002 — The Real Meaning**
> Template: `"What does it actually mean when [common_trigger] happens?"`
> Example: `"What does it actually mean when your iPhone says 'This accessory may not be supported'?"`
> When to use: When the scam exploits a familiar but misunderstood system behaviour.
> Psychology: Knowledge gap + relevance. The viewer has seen this but never understood it.

**CUR-003 — The Impossible Claim**
> Template: `"Can scammers really [capability_viewer_doubts]?"`
> Example: `"Can scammers really clone your voice from a 3-second audio clip?"`
> When to use: When the scam uses a tactic that sounds implausible but is real.
> Psychology: Disbelief + curiosity. The viewer wants this to be false but needs to check.

**CUR-004 — The Hidden Step**
> Template: `"What happens after you [common_safe_seeming_action]?"`
> Example: `"What happens after you click 'unsubscribe' on a suspicious email?"`
> When to use: When a normal, seemingly safe action triggers a scam.
> Psychology: Assumption challenge. The action they thought was safe is actually dangerous.

**CUR-005 — The Test**
> Template: `"Can you spot [scam_element] before I show you what it is?"`
> Example: `"Can you spot the fake in this bank email before I show you what to look for?"`
> When to use: When the scam can be visualised — a fake email, a spoofed number, a cloned website.
> Psychology: Challenge + game mechanics. The viewer becomes an active participant, not a passive learner.

**CUR-006 — The Number**
> Template: `"What do you think happens when scammers get hold of just your [data_type]?"`
> Example: `"What do you think happens when scammers get hold of just your sort code and account number?"`
> When to use: When the scam requires less information than the viewer expects.
> Psychology: Underestimation gap. The viewer is about to discover they are more exposed than they knew.

**CUR-007 — The Process Question**
> Template: `"Have you ever wondered why scam calls always [specific_pattern]?"`
> Example: `"Have you ever wondered why scam calls always seem to know your name?"`
> When to use: When the scam has a recognisable pattern the viewer has noticed but never understood.
> Psychology: Validates existing observation + adds new meaning. The viewer thinks "Yes, actually — why?".

**CUR-008 — The Reveal Setup** *(new)*
> Template: `"Most people fail this test. Here is the question: [scam_scenario]."`
> Example: `"Most people fail this test. Here is the question: a text from your bank says your account is locked — what do you do?"`
> When to use: When the scam relies on a predictable victim mistake that the viewer will also make.
> Psychology: Ego + curiosity. The viewer is confident they know the answer — and about to be surprised.

### CATEGORY 4: PERSONAL / RELATABLE HOOKS
*Best for: Scams that target everyday behaviour. The viewer recognises themselves in the scenario.*
*Psychology trigger: Identity relevance + Fear of loss*

---

**PER-001 — The Scenario**
> Template: `"You receive a message from [trusted_sender]. You [natural_response]. But [twist]."`
> Example: `"You receive a message from your son. You click the link to help him. But it is not your son."`
> When to use: When the scam impersonates a trusted contact.
> Psychology: Story entry. The viewer is immediately inside the scenario — first-person stakes.

**PER-002 — The Everyday Action**
> Template: `"Every time you [common_digital_action], scammers are waiting for this one mistake."`
> Example: `"Every time you connect to airport WiFi, scammers are waiting for this one mistake."`
> When to use: When the scam exploits a habit the viewer does regularly without thinking.
> Psychology: Vulnerability + relevance. The viewer's daily routine is the attack surface.

**PER-003 — The Family Story**
> Template: `"My [family_member] almost lost everything to this scam. Here is what happened."`
> Example: `"My grandmother almost lost her savings to this scam. Here is what happened."`
> When to use: When the scam has high financial or emotional impact on older adults.
> Psychology: Personal narrative + social proof. Story format creates emotional investment.
> ⚠️ Use only when the script can authentically deliver this without feeling fabricated.

**PER-004 — The Mistake**
> Template: `"I used to think [incorrect_assumption]. Then I learnt about [scam_type]."`
> Example: `"I used to think I could always spot a fake email. Then I learnt about pixel-perfect phishing pages."`
> When to use: When the scam exploits overconfidence in one's own scam-detection ability.
> Psychology: Shared mistake. The viewer's assumption is validated then challenged — with them in the scene.

**PER-005 — The Direct Address**
> Template: `"If you use [specific_platform_or_service] — this message is for you specifically."`
> Example: `"If you use online banking on your phone — this message is for you specifically."`
> When to use: When the scam targets a specific but common digital behaviour.
> Psychology: Identity filter. The viewer feels selected — not part of a mass audience, but directly addressed.

**PER-006 — The Moment of Vulnerability**
> Template: `"Scammers target you when you are [specific_vulnerable_state]. Here is how."`
> Example: `"Scammers target you when you are waiting for a delivery. Here is how they know."`
> When to use: When the scam exploits a predictable emotional or behavioural state.
> Psychology: Empathy + fear of loss. Acknowledges a real moment the viewer has experienced.

**PER-007 — The Assumption**
> Template: `"You would never fall for a scam — right? Here is what the research shows."`
> Example: `"You would never fall for a phone scam — right? Here is what the research shows about who does."`
> When to use: When the scam targets educated, confident adults who consider themselves too smart to be fooled.
> Psychology: Ego challenge. The viewer's confidence is the very thing that makes them vulnerable.

**PER-008 — The Victim Profile** *(new)*
> Template: `"The typical victim of this scam is [unexpected_profile]. Here is why."`
> Example: `"The typical victim of this scam is a university-educated adult in their 40s. Here is why."`
> When to use: When Googly's research reveals a surprising victim demographic.
> Psychology: Assumption challenge + identity relevance. Breaks the stereotype of who gets scammed.

### CATEGORY 5: OHN SIGNATURE HOOKS
*Best for: OHN's most distinctive content style — first-person, high-stakes, deeply personal.*
*Psychology trigger: Betrayal of trust + Identity relevance*
*Note: These are OHN's proprietary style. Use sparingly — one per batch maximum.*

---

**OHN-001 — The Trusted Sender Betrayal**
> Template: `"Scammers are sending messages FROM [trusted_contact_type]'s number. Not pretending to be them — using their actual account."`
> Example: `"Scammers are sending messages FROM your mum's WhatsApp. Not pretending to be her — using her actual account."`
> When to use: When the scam compromises a real contact's account (e.g. WhatsApp account takeover).
> Psychology: Maximum betrayal of trust. The safe channel (a contact you know) has been weaponised.
> ✅ Proven: Structural basis for OHN's highest-retention transcript format.

**OHN-002 — The Safety Net Failure**
> Template: `"The thing you do to stay safe online? Scammers have found a way around it."`
> Example: `"Two-factor authentication — the thing you do to stay safe? Scammers have found a way around it."`
> When to use: When the scam specifically defeats a well-known protection (2FA, caller ID, email filters).
> Psychology: Assumption challenge + betrayal. The viewer's safety behaviour is no longer enough.

**OHN-003 — The Invisible Scam**
> Template: `"You can be targeted by this scam without ever clicking a link, opening an email, or answering a call."`
> Example: `"You can be a victim of SIM swapping without ever clicking a link, opening an email, or answering a call."`
> When to use: When the scam requires zero victim action — it happens entirely on the infrastructure side.
> Psychology: Helplessness + urgency. Removes the viewer's sense of control — and makes the solution more valuable.

**OHN-004 — The Known Sender**
> Template: `"A message arrives from [real_institution]. It is on the same thread as their real messages. And it is fake."`
> Example: `"A text arrives from your bank. It is on the same thread as every real text they have ever sent you. And it is fake."`
> When to use: When the scam exploits SMS thread spoofing (the fake and real messages appear in the same thread).
> Psychology: Maximum cognitive dissonance. The visual evidence of safety (same thread) is itself the trap.

**OHN-005 — The Moment Before**
> Template: `"Thirty seconds from now, you could be sending a scammer your life savings — and thinking it is the right thing to do."`
> Example: `"Thirty seconds from now, you could be transferring money to a scammer — and believing you are protecting your account."`
> When to use: When the scam uses psychological pressure to manufacture urgency (authorised push payment fraud).
> Psychology: Temporal proximity. The threat is not abstract — it is 30 seconds from now.

**OHN-006 — The Face You Trust**
> Template: `"What if the next video call from [trusted_person] is not actually [trusted_person]?"`
> Example: `"What if the next video call from your bank manager is not actually your bank manager?"`
> When to use: When the scam uses deepfake video (AI-generated face/voice on a video call).
> Psychology: Identity threat at maximum level. Even face-to-face visual confirmation is now unsafe.

**OHN-007 — The Price of Normal**
> Template: `"Doing [completely_normal_thing] is now enough for scammers to [devastating_outcome]."`
> Example: `"Posting a photo on Instagram is now enough for scammers to clone your voice and call your parents."`
> When to use: When the scam harvests data from normal social media behaviour.
> Psychology: Normalcy as vulnerability. The viewer is being passively targeted by doing nothing unusual.

**OHN-008 — The Trusted Tool** *(new)*
> Template: `"[Trusted_technology] was designed to protect you. Scammers have turned it into the attack."`
> Example: `"Caller ID was designed to protect you. Scammers have turned it into the attack."`
> When to use: When a security feature (caller ID, 2FA SMS, email filters) is itself weaponised.
> Psychology: Deep betrayal of trust. The protection mechanism is the vulnerability.

### CATEGORY 6: CONTRAST / MYTH-BUSTING HOOKS *(new category)*
*Best for: Scams that exploit what people THINK protects them. The viewer holds a false belief.*
*Psychology trigger: Assumption challenge*

---

**CON-001 — The False Safety**
> Template: `"Scams that start with [thing_viewer_thinks_is_safe] are now the most dangerous type."`
> Example: `"Scams that start with a real phone call from your actual bank are now the most dangerous type."`
> When to use: When the scam specifically begins with something the viewer considers a safety signal.
> Psychology: Inverts the safety hierarchy. The thing they trusted most is now the red flag.

**CON-002 — The Myth**
> Template: `"'[Common_safety_belief].' This is not true anymore."`
> Example: `"'If the website has a padlock, it is safe.' This is not true anymore."`
> When to use: When the scam exploits an outdated safety rule that people still follow.
> Psychology: Direct myth-busting. Short, punchy, and contradicts a concrete belief.

**CON-003 — The Comparison**
> Template: `"Scammers do not want you to know the difference between [real_thing] and [fake_thing]."`
> Example: `"Scammers do not want you to know the difference between a real HMRC letter and a fake one. Here is what it is."`
> When to use: When the scam closely mimics a legitimate communication and the difference is subtle.
> Psychology: Insider access. The viewer is about to receive information scammers want suppressed.

**CON-004 — The Inversion**
> Template: `"The more [positive_trait] you are, the more vulnerable you are to this scam."`
> Example: `"The more helpful you are, the more vulnerable you are to this scam."`
> When to use: When the scam specifically targets helpful, trusting, or conscientious people.
> Psychology: Identity challenge. The viewer's positive qualities are reframed as vulnerabilities.

**CON-005 — The Updated Rule** *(new)*
> Template: `"The old rule was: [old_advice]. The new rule is: [new_advice_that_contradicts_it]."`
> Example: `"The old rule was: never click links in emails. The new rule is: even typing the URL yourself isn't safe."`
> When to use: When scammers have evolved past the standard advice people follow.
> Psychology: Urgency + assumption challenge. Prior knowledge is now a false sense of security.

**CON-006 — The Backwards Assumption** *(new)*
> Template: `"You think you can spot a scammer. The research shows scammers are better at spotting you."`
> Example: `"You think you can spot a scammer. The research shows scammers profile victims before making contact."`
> When to use: When the scam involves research or targeting of specific victim types before contact.
> Psychology: Power inversion. The viewer is not the hunter — they are the prey. Resets overconfidence.

### CATEGORY 7: INSIDER KNOWLEDGE HOOKS *(new category)*
*Best for: Scams with a hidden mechanism that most people don't know exists. The viewer is about to learn something exclusive.*
*Psychology trigger: Insider access*

---

**INS-001 — The Playbook**
> Template: `"Here is the exact script scammers use when they call you pretending to be [institution]."`
> Example: `"Here is the exact script scammers use when they call you pretending to be HMRC."`
> When to use: When Googly's research reveals the specific wording or sequence scammers use.
> Psychology: Insider access + preparation. Knowing the script removes the scammer's power.

**INS-002 — The Trick They Use**
> Template: `"Scammers use one psychological trick in almost every phone call. Here is what it is."`
> Example: `"Scammers use one psychological trick in almost every phone call. It makes you feel like you have no choice. Here is what it is."`
> When to use: When the scam relies on a specific psychological manipulation technique (urgency, authority, isolation).
> Psychology: Demystification. Understanding the trick removes its effect.

**INS-003 — The Tell**
> Template: `"There is one thing every [scam_type] has in common. Once you see it, you cannot unsee it."`
> Example: `"There is one thing every fake bank website has in common. Once you see it, you cannot unsee it."`
> When to use: When the scam has a consistent, learnable tell that the viewer can immediately apply.
> Psychology: Curiosity gap + insider access. Promises a permanent upgrade to the viewer's scam detection.

**INS-004 — The Word They Use**
> Template: `"There is one word scammers almost always use in the first 10 seconds of a call. Here it is."`
> Example: `"There is one word scammers almost always use in the first 10 seconds of a call. It is designed to stop you thinking clearly."`
> When to use: When the scam involves a phone call with a recognisable linguistic pattern.
> Psychology: Hyper-specific insider knowledge. A single word is memorable and immediately applicable.

**INS-005 — The Sequence** *(new)*
> Template: `"If [event_1] happens, then [event_2] happens — you are inside a scam. Here is what each step is doing to your brain."`
> Example: `"If a call comes from your bank, then they ask you to move money to a 'safe account' — you are inside an authorised push payment scam. Here is what each step is doing to your brain."`
> When to use: When the scam has a distinct multi-step sequence that can be recognised in real time.
> Psychology: Pattern recognition. Once the viewer knows the sequence, they can self-interrupt at any step.

**INS-006 — The Insider Reveal** *(new)*
> Template: `"A former scammer explained exactly how they chose their victims. Here is what they said."`
> Example: `"A former phone scammer described the type of person they targeted first. Here is what they said."`
> When to use: When Googly's research includes first-person accounts from reformed scammers or law enforcement interviews.
> Psychology: Maximum insider access. A scammer's perspective is the most credible possible source on how to avoid scammers.
> ⚠️ Only use if Googly has a verified source for this — never fabricate a scammer quote.

## Category Index

Quick reference for Scripty to select the right category by scam type:

| Scam Type | Primary Category | Secondary Category |
|---|---|---|
| Trusted tool weaponised (WhatsApp, Gmail, etc.) | REVELATION | OHN SIGNATURE |
| Rapidly spreading / trending | WARNING/URGENCY | REVELATION |
| Counterintuitive or surprising mechanism | CURIOSITY/QUESTION | INSIDER KNOWLEDGE |
| Targets everyday behaviour | PERSONAL/RELATABLE | CONTRAST/MYTH-BUSTING |
| Defeats a known protection | OHN SIGNATURE | CONTRAST/MYTH-BUSTING |
| Hidden or underreported | REVELATION | INSIDER KNOWLEDGE |
