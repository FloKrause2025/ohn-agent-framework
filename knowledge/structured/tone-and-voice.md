## What This KB Is For

This KB defines how OHN sounds. The script-structure KB defines what goes in each part. This KB defines how every sentence must feel. Use it before writing and to self-check after writing.

## Why the Agent Needs This

Without a voice reference, scripts drift toward the two failure modes that kill OHN content: (1) institutional/leaflet language that sounds like a government pamphlet, and (2) alarmist/fear-mongering language that sounds like clickbait. OHN sits between both extremes — a very specific register that is hard to hit without a documented reference.

> **If ignored:** Scripts sound corporate, preachy, or generic. Viewer trust drops. Share rate drops. The format becomes indistinguishable from any other scam-awareness content.

---

## The OHN Voice Identity

OHN sounds like **a trusted friend who happens to know a lot about online scams** — not a teacher, not a news anchor, not a cybersecurity expert. A friend who just found out something alarming and genuinely needs to tell you right now.

### The voice IS:
- Plainspoken and direct — says what it means, fewest words possible
- Calm but urgent — never panicked, never alarmist
- Caring — the viewer leaves feeling smarter and safer, not scared and helpless
- Slightly irreverent — not corporate, has personality

### The voice is NOT:
- Fear-mongering — `"URGENT: You could lose EVERYTHING tomorrow!"`
- Academic or technical — `"Multi-factor authentication via TOTP protocols..."`
- Preachy or condescending — `"If you're a responsible person, you should..."`
- Corporate or generic — `"Stay safe online with these helpful tips"`
- Broadcaster-formal — `"Good afternoon. Today we examine a growing threat..."`

### The Voice Test
> Read every sentence aloud. If it sounds like a government leaflet — rewrite it.
> If it sounds like you're telling your mum something important over coffee — it is ready.

## Sentence Rhythm

OHN scripts are written for the ear, not the page. They are spoken aloud by a real person. Rhythm matters as much as words.

### The Rhythm Rule
Short sentences create impact. Longer sentences carry the story. Mix them deliberately. Never chop every sentence to the same length — that produces robotic, lifeless writing.

```
❌ BAD RHYTHM (robotic):
"Scammers send you a text. The text looks real. It says your bank needs you.
You should not click the link. It is a fake website."

✅ GOOD RHYTHM (human):
"You get a text from your bank. It looks completely real — same logo, same language,
same urgency. But here's the thing. That link doesn't go to your bank."
```

### Rhythm Patterns by Section

**Context (storytelling rhythm):** Start slow. Conversational. Place the viewer in a normal moment. Slightly longer sentences that feel like storytelling.
```
✅ "You're checking your emails. Nothing unusual."
✅ "One day your phone randomly loses all signal. Zero bars. You think it's just a bad area."
```

**Tension (pressure rhythm):** Build pressure. Mix short punches with one longer sentence that tightens the stakes.
```
✅ "You can't make calls. You can't load Instagram. But the real problem is your phone number."
✅ "They already have your sort code. Your name. Your date of birth."
```

**Pivot (reveal rhythm):** Short, punchy sentences for the reveal. Then one slightly longer sentence to explain the consequence. The "aha" moment should land in under 10 words.
```
✅ "It's not a high-tech hack. The scammer just calls your mobile provider. They pretend to be you."
✅ "Then BOOM. A second email arrives. This one is fake."
```

**Payoff (instruction rhythm):** Friend telling you what to do. Short, specific, direct. Two sentences per tip — one names the action, one explains why.
```
✅ "Call your mobile provider today. Set a Transfer Pin or Port-Out Lock."
✅ "Never confirm an email you didn't request."
```

### The Spoken Test
After writing each section — read it aloud. Time it. If you stumble, the sentence is too long or awkward. If it flows naturally when spoken, it is ready.

## Banned Words and Phrases

Hard stops. Never use these in any script.

| Banned | Why | Use Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Senior" or "elderly" | Othering language — the audience does not identify this way | "parents", "grandparents", "older adults" |
| "Hacker" (dramatic sense) | Incorrect framing — OHN covers scammers, not system hackers | "scammer" (max 2× per script), "they", "these people", "criminals" |
| "Cyber" anything | Jargon ("cybersecurity", "cybercrime") | Rephrase in plain language |
| "Hey guys" / "Welcome back" / "Hi everyone" | Signals a broadcast format, not a friend | Nothing — start the script instantly |
| "Stay safe out there" / "Thanks for watching" | Generic sign-off — the script ends after the Payoff CTA | Nothing — end at the CTA |
| "Guarantee" | Never promise outcomes | Remove or rephrase |
| "Most likely" / "might be" / "could potentially" | Hedging kills urgency in the Pivot | Definitive language only — "It IS a scam", not "it might be" |
| "If you're a good person" | Preachy and condescending | Remove entirely |
| "Unfortunately" (as sentence opener) | Passive and weak | Replace with a direct statement |
| "Scammer" more than twice | Repetition sounds robotic | Vary: "they", "these people", "criminals" |
| Product pitch inside script | Destroys trust — feels like bait-and-switch | Never. CTA is about family protection only. |

## Section Voice Reference

### Hook Voice
Direct. Specific. No warmup. The first word should feel like the viewer is already mid-conversation.

```
❌ "Today, I want to tell you about a scam that a lot of people are falling for."
✅ "Scammers have found a way to open fake PayPal accounts using your Gmail address."
```

### Context Voice
Warm and conversational. Place the viewer inside a normal, recognisable moment. Read like a story being told, not a news report being delivered.

```
❌ "Many consumers report receiving fraudulent communications purporting to be from their bank."
✅ "You get a text from your bank. It looks completely real."
```

### Tension Voice
Urgent but controlled. Second person, present tense. Specific. The viewer should feel slightly uncomfortable — that is the goal. Never panicked. Never preachy.

```
❌ "This is extremely dangerous and you should be very worried."
✅ "While you sit there wondering why your signal is dead — they already have your number."
```

### Pivot Voice
Authoritative and clear. Short sentences. Definitive. The viewer should feel like they are receiving insider knowledge from someone who knows exactly how this works.

```
❌ "The link probably leads to a fake website designed to look like your bank's login page."
✅ "That link goes to a pixel-perfect copy of your bank's login page. Type your password. They have it."
```

### Payoff Voice
Friend voice throughout. Warm, direct, specific. Action + reason for each tip. Never switches to institutional language at the end.

```
❌ "Enable two-factor authentication for enhanced account security."
✅ "Turn on two-step login in your Gmail settings. It means even if they have your password — they still can't get in."
```

## Caption Hook Voice

Every script includes a caption hook — the first line of the Instagram caption. Different rules from the script hook:

- Can be slightly longer (up to 20 words)
- Should hint at the payoff without giving it away
- Must work as a standalone statement with no video context
- Sparks curiosity from a cold scroll

```
✅ "You've been confirming scammer accounts without knowing it."  (Gmail Dot Scam)
✅ "Your phone losing signal could mean scammers just stole your SIM."  (SIM Swapping)
✅ "It came from your contact. That is what makes it so dangerous."  (WhatsApp Phishing)
```
