## What This KB Is For

This KB gives Scripty and Quality Gate three high-performing OHN transcripts (with performance data and analysis) and one low-performing transcript (with a full failure autopsy). Together they define the performance ceiling and the failure floor for every script.

## Why the Agent Needs This

Structural rules tell Scripty what to do. Real examples show what "correct" looks like in practice. The gap between following the rules and writing a genuinely high-performing script is filled by pattern recognition — recognising what a good Pivot feels like, what a rhythm break sounds like, what a real Visual Anchor looks like. These transcripts provide that pattern library.

> **If ignored:** Scripts that follow the rules technically but miss the feel. The structure is correct but the energy is flat. Performance data exists in the codebase — use it.

---

## How to Use This KB

**Scripty:** Before writing — read all 3 high-performing transcripts. Identify the rhythm, the Visual Anchors, the tension scene, and the payoff structure. Then write. After writing — check the low-performing transcript against your draft. Does your draft have any of its failure patterns?

**Quality Gate:** Use the performance data as the benchmark. A script with the same structural quality as the Gmail Dot Scam should score 9–10. A script with any of the Marketplace failure patterns should be flagged immediately.

## HIGH-PERFORMING TRANSCRIPT 1 — Gmail Dot Scam

### Performance Data
| Metric | Value | Benchmark Status |
|---|---|---|
| Views | 228,541 | — |
| 3-Second Retention | 75% | ⭐ Exceptional |
| Average Watch Time % | 42% | 🟡 Acceptable |
| Share Rate | 8.9% | ⭐ Exceptional |
| Save Rate | 3.4% | 🟢 Strong |
| Skip Rate | 29.9% | — |

### Why It Worked
- **Revelation hook** — the dot trick is genuinely counterintuitive. Nobody expects dots to be irrelevant in a Gmail address. The mechanism surprises even technically savvy viewers.
- **75% 3-second retention** — highest hook performance in the OHN library. Combined with the pre-hook visual moment (see below), viewers had two reasons to stop scrolling before the spoken hook finished.
- **8.9% share rate** — highest in the OHN library. Counterintuitive mechanics drive shares: when people learn something that genuinely surprises them, their first instinct is to warn the people they love.
- **Universal audience** — everyone has a Gmail account. The scenario is immediately relevant to 100% of viewers.
- **Strong "aha" moment in Pivot** — the dot trick is simple enough to explain in one sentence but surprising enough to feel like a revelation. The viewer feels smart for understanding it.
- **Specific, actionable Payoff** — "Go to PayPal and check right now" gives the viewer one clear, immediate action.

### Pre-Hook Performance Factor (critical)
This video had a 1-second unscripted pre-hook before the spoken hook began. One of the OHN founders was filmed from the side saying *"No way it works..."* while testing the dot trick on PayPal in real time — capturing his genuine reaction as it happened.

This pre-hook almost certainly drove the exceptional 75% 3-second retention. It worked because it contained all 3 C's before a single scripted word was spoken:
- **Clarity:** Someone is clearly testing something on screen
- **Context:** A real person, a real reaction — feels authentic, not staged
- **Curiosity:** "No way it works..." — works? What works? The viewer must find out

> **Lesson:** Authentic unscripted visual moments captured before the spoken hook begins can significantly boost 3-second retention. This cannot be forced or scripted — the moment must be genuine. Scripty must always include the Pre-Hook Note in Part 1.

### The Script
```
PRE-HOOK NOTE: If an authentic, unscripted reaction moment was captured during filming —
place it here before the spoken hook begins.

HOOK:
"Scammers have a way to use your own Gmail address to open fake accounts — just by adding a dot. Here's how."

CONTEXT:
Let's say your email is peterpan@gmail.com.
Scammers bought your email from the dark web.
So they go to PayPal and create a new account.
But they use peter.pan@gmail.com — with a dot.

TENSION:
Here's the thing — dots don't matter in Gmail.
Google says so directly in their help centre.
So YOU still receive every email sent to that dotted address.
You're clueless. Until one day you get an email from PayPal.

PIVOT:
It's official. From service.paypal.com.
Asking you to confirm your email address.
You're confused — but you click confirm.
Then BOOM. A second email arrives. This one is fake.
Because you already verified the first, you're less suspicious.
They ask for your password. And they get in.

PAYOFF:
Here's what to do.
Go to PayPal and check if any accounts use a dotted version of your email.
If yes — report it immediately.
And never confirm an email you didn't request.

CAPTION HOOK: "You've been confirming scammer accounts without knowing it."
WORD COUNT: 148
```

### Curiosity Loop Trace
| Part | Loop Status | What the Viewer Asks |
|---|---|---|
| Hook | OPENED | "A dot in my Gmail address? How does that work?" |
| Context | MAINTAINED | "Someone created an account with a dotted version — so what?" |

| Pivot | BUILDING | "I understand the trick now — but am I already affected?" |

## HIGH-PERFORMING TRANSCRIPT 2 — SIM Swapping Scam

### Performance Data
| Metric | Value | Benchmark Status |
|---|---|---|
| Views | 83,277 | — |
| 3-Second Retention | 71% | 🟢 Strong |
| Average Watch Time % | 42.7% | 🟡 Acceptable |
| Share Rate | 3.6% | 🟡 Acceptable |
| Save Rate | 5.9% | ⭐ Exceptional |
| Skip Rate | 26.6% | — |

### Why It Worked
- **Best save rate of all videos (5.9%)** — the Payoff delivered a specific, little-known action (Transfer Pin / Port-Out Lock) that viewers wanted to save and act on later. High save rate = high perceived value of the content.
- **Unique question hook format** — "If we asked you which option is safer..." challenges the viewer's existing belief rather than just revealing new information. Highly engaging and underused pattern.
- **Best watch time % (42.7%)** — the body held attention consistently. The Tension connected to something universally feared: losing access to your bank account.
- **Lowest skip rate of all videos (26.6%)** — viewers did not abandon this video. Pacing and sentence structure kept them engaged hook to payoff.
- **Exclusive-feeling Payoff** — "Transfer Pin" and "Port-Out Lock" are terms most people have never heard. Viewers feel they received insider knowledge unavailable elsewhere.

### The Script
```
PRE-HOOK NOTE: If an authentic, unscripted reaction moment was captured during filming —
place it here before the spoken hook begins.

HOOK:
"If we asked you which option is safer to receive a security code — what would you say?"

CONTEXT:
One day your phone randomly loses all signal.
Zero bars. You think it's just a bad area.
But if the signal never comes back — scammers may have already stolen your SIM.

TENSION:
You can't make calls. You can't load Instagram.
But the real problem is your phone number.
It's connected to your 2-Factor Authentication on every account.
Your bank sends security codes by text to log in.
If scammers have your SIM — they get those codes.
They can reset every password you own in minutes.
While you sit there wondering why your signal is dead.

PIVOT:
Here's the thing — it's not a high-tech hack.
The scammer just calls your mobile provider.
They pretend to be you.
They use your name, address, and the last four digits of your Social Security Number.
All bought on the dark web for less than a coffee.
They say: "I lost my phone, I need a new SIM immediately."
The customer service agent just wants to help.

PAYOFF:
Call your mobile provider today.
Set a Transfer Pin or Port-Out Lock.
This requires a secret password before any SIM changes — even if they have all your details.
Then stop using your phone number for Multi-Factor Authentication.
Switch to an Authenticator App like Google or Microsoft.
If scammers steal your SIM now — they get nothing.

CAPTION HOOK: "Your phone losing signal could mean scammers just stole your SIM."
WORD COUNT: 158
```

### Key Pattern to Study
The Tension in this script uses the **"You Can't..." pattern** extended across 7 sentences — each stacking a new consequence before landing on the worst one: "they can reset every password you own in minutes." This is the escalation pattern at its best. Personal threat first. Family impact second. Financial devastation last.

## HIGH-PERFORMING TRANSCRIPT 3 — WhatsApp Phishing Scam

### Performance Data
| Metric | Value | Benchmark Status |
|---|---|---|
| Views | 252,582 | — |
| 3-Second Retention | 77% | ⭐ Exceptional |
| Average Watch Time % | 30.4% | 🔴 Below |
| Share Rate | 4.2% | 🟢 Strong |
| Save Rate | 1.3% | 🟡 Acceptable |
| Skip Rate | 26.5% | — |

### Why It Worked
- **Highest reach of all videos (252k views)** — broadest audience appeal. WhatsApp is universally used across all age groups.
- **Best 3-second hook retention (77%)** — strongest opening hook in the entire OHN library. The threat coming from a trusted contact makes it immediately personal and urgent.
- **High share count (10,612)** — people shared this to warn their own contacts. The "it came from someone you trust" mechanic is inherently shareable.

### What Could Be Improved (critical lesson)
- **Lowest watch time % of the three (30.4%)** — the body of the script lost viewers after the strong hook. The hook created high expectations the middle sections did not fully sustain.
- **Largest gap between hook retention and watch time** — 77% hook retention vs 30.4% average watch time is the biggest drop-off in the library. The hook worked brilliantly but the Tension or Pivot lost urgency or became too complex.

> **Critical Lesson:** A great hook is wasted if the body does not match its energy and clarity. The hook sets a promise. Every section after it must keep that promise at the same intensity. If the hook is a 10/10 — the Context, Tension, Pivot, and Payoff must also be 10/10. **Never let the energy drop after the hook lands.**

### The Script
```
PRE-HOOK NOTE: If an authentic, unscripted reaction moment was captured during filming —
place it here before the spoken hook begins.

HOOK:
"Scammers are claiming they have photos of you to gain control of your WhatsApp account. Here's how."

CONTEXT:
This scam is rising fast.
Because it comes from one of YOUR contacts.
Not a random number — someone you already trust.
Someone whose account scammers already control.

TENSION:
This creates two massive threats.
First: YOUR privacy.
They can read every message, photo, and document in your account.
Including any banking information.
Second: YOUR contacts.
They send the same scam to your parents and grandparents.

PIVOT:
Here's what the message looks like.
It sends you a link that opens a fake Facebook login page.
You enter your phone number.
Instantly you get two notifications.
The first is from WhatsApp — asking to link a new device.
The second is on the fake site — giving you a code to enter.
Once you enter that code, scammers have full control.

PAYOFF:
Open WhatsApp right now.
Go to Settings — Linked Devices.
Tap any unfamiliar device and log it out immediately.
And if a contact sends you a suspicious link — call them to verify before clicking.

CAPTION HOOK: "It came from your contact. That is what makes it so dangerous."
WORD COUNT: 152
```

## LOW-PERFORMING TRANSCRIPT — Marketplace Refund Scam

### Performance Data
| Metric | Value | Benchmark Status |
|---|---|---|
| Views | 2,274 | — |
| 3-Second Retention | 41% | 🔴 Below |
| Average Watch Time % | 14.1% | 🔴 Below |
| Share Rate | 0.4% | 🔴 Below |
| Save Rate | 0.26% | 🔴 Below |
| Skip Rate | 63.9% | — |

### Failure Summary
This script broke almost every rule simultaneously. The result: 41% 3-second retention, 14.1% watch time, 63.9% skip rate, 0.4% share rate. The worst-performing video in the OHN library.

Study every failure below. These are not edge cases — they are the most common mistakes in scam-awareness content.

### Failure Autopsy (8 failures)

**❌ FAILURE 1 — Hook too long (30+ words, nearly 3× the 15-word limit)**
Most viewers scrolled before the hook even finished. This alone explains the 41% 3-second retention.
> Rule violated: Hook must be 10–15 words maximum.

**❌ FAILURE 2 — Hook too vague ("THIS one thing")**
"THIS one thing" has zero Clarity. The viewer has no idea what the scam is. Vague hooks do not stop the scroll.
> Rule violated: Clarity is non-negotiable. The viewer must know exactly what this is within 5 seconds.

**❌ FAILURE 3 — Hook excluded the audience ("Imagine your mom lists a couch")**
Only speaks to people who sell items online. Immediately excluded the majority of viewers before the first sentence ended.
> Rule violated: Every hook must be relevant to ANYONE who uses the internet.

**❌ FAILURE 4 — Structure collapsed (THREE tension sections instead of one)**
There is ONE tension section. Multiple tension sections destroy pacing and confuse the viewer — they lose track of where the story is going.
> Rule violated: One Tension section only. Never split.

**❌ FAILURE 5 — Preachy language in Tension ("If you're a good person")**
Condescending. It implies the viewer might not be a good person. Breaks trust immediately. Never moralize.
> Rule violated: Never be preachy or condescending.

**❌ FAILURE 6 — Uncertain language in Pivot ("most likely FAKE")**
"Most likely" kills urgency. Either it is fake or it is not. If it is a scam — say it is. Uncertainty signals the creator isn't sure either — so why should the viewer keep watching?
> Rule violated: Definitive language only. Never hedge in the Pivot.

**❌ FAILURE 7 — Payoff had four tips (maximum is 3)**
Four tips overwhelm the viewer and cause drop-off at the most important moment of the script.
> Rule violated: Maximum 3 Payoff tips. Never 4.

**❌ FAILURE 8 — Closing section and sign-off added (neither exists in OHN structure)**
A product pitch and follow request were added after the Payoff. The script ends at the Payoff CTA. Always. A product pitch inside a 60-second educational script feels like a bait-and-switch and destroys viewer trust.
> Rule violated: No Closing section. No sign-off. The script ends after the Payoff.

### The Script (annotated)
```
HOOK (❌ TOO LONG, TOO VAGUE, EXCLUDES AUDIENCE):
"The second a Marketplace buyer says THIS one thing, they are trying to rob you.
Here's how the scam works and what to do about it."
→ 30+ words. "THIS one thing" has no Clarity. "Marketplace buyer" excludes most viewers.

CONTEXT:
"Imagine your mom lists a couch. Or your dad is selling his old iPhone.
A buyer messages right away and is showing interest.
You have a friendly back and forth chat before they agree to make the
payment via PayPal Venmo or Zelle."

TENSION 1 (❌ FIRST OF THREE — STRUCTURE ALREADY BROKEN):
"Then a confirmation email hits your inbox.
It says that the buyer transferred you the money but you notice
something is off...?
The amount is wrong."

TENSION 2 (❌ SECOND TENSION SECTION — DOES NOT EXIST):
"Right after you get the email, a message pops up again from the buyer.
They say they just accidentally sent too much and ask you to refund
the difference or in some cases the full total back."

TENSION 3 (❌ THIRD TENSION SECTION + PREACHY LANGUAGE):
"If you're a good person, you might consider at least sending the difference.
BUT STOP - this could be a scam and here's how it works."
→ "If you're a good person" is condescending. Never write this.

PIVOT (❌ UNCERTAIN LANGUAGE — "MOST LIKELY"):
"The confirmation email you received about the payment is most likely FAKE.
Meaning money was actually never sent to your account."
→ "Most likely" = hedging. Either it is fake or it is not.

PAYOFF (❌ FOUR TIPS — MAXIMUM IS 3, GRAMMAR ERRORS):
"First: Check the senders email address. If its not an official email
address - immediately you know it's a scam.
Second: Log into the official APP of the payment provider you are using
and check for any transactions. If there are none - you can be sure its a scam.
Third: Advise the buyer to contact the payment provider to solve this
issue by themselves.
Fourth: The most important part: Block the scammer from the Marketplace!"
→ Four tips. "Advise the buyer to... by themselves" = grammar error. Destroys credibility.

CLOSING SECTION (❌ DOES NOT EXIST IN OHN STRUCTURE):
"As great as these marketplaces are, they are dangerous places to get scammed.
Unfortunately, our parents are more likely to sell and buy stuff on the
marketplace and are therefore the prime targets. For that reason we have
built a Scam Defense Starter Kit for our parents to minimize the chances
to get scammed."
→ Product pitch. Never allowed inside the script.

SIGN-OFF (❌ DOES NOT EXIST IN OHN STRUCTURE):
"Oh ya - and follow us if you want to see part 2-3-4-5 of our Refund Scam series."
→ Follow request inside the script. Worst possible ending.

WORD COUNT: 271 (limit is 150 — nearly double)
```
