## What This KB Is For

This KB gives Scripty the complete structural rules for writing a 60-second OHN scam-awareness video script. It defines the 5 parts, their exact function, the curiosity loop they must maintain, the transition phrases between parts, and the failure modes for each.

## Why the Agent Needs This

Without a documented structure, scripts become free-form — some parts run too long, some are skipped, the tension becomes a list instead of a scene, and the payoff switches from human voice to institutional leaflet language. This KB is the structural contract every script must honour.

> **If ignored:** Inconsistent scripts. Weak pivots. Preachy tension. Viewer drop-off at second 20. The algorithm punishes what the structure prevents.

---

## The Curiosity Loop — Master Concept

Every OHN script is built around a **Curiosity Loop**: a question opened in the Hook that the viewer's brain cannot move on without answering. Every part of the script either opens, maintains, deepens, or closes this loop.

| Part | Loop Role | What the Viewer Asks |
|---|---|---|
| Hook | OPENS the loop | "Wait — how does that work?" |
| Context | MAINTAINS the loop | "OK, I'm in the scene — but when does the scam hit?" |
| Tension | DEEPENS the loop | "This could happen to ME — but HOW do I stop it?" |
| Pivot | BUILDS toward closure | "I understand the trick — but what do I DO?" |
| Payoff | CLOSES the loop | "I can do this today. I should share this." |

> **Rule:** If any part of the script resolves the loop too early — the viewer has no reason to keep watching. Every part must leave one open question until the next part.

## The 5-Part Structure

---

### PART 1 — HOOK
**Duration:** 3–5 seconds spoken | **Word count:** 10–15 words maximum

#### What It Does
Opens the curiosity loop. Gives the viewer a specific, personal reason to keep watching. No warmup, no greeting, no context — straight into the knowledge gap.

#### Rules
- Start instantly — no greeting, no name, no "Hey guys"
- Maximum 15 words — count them before submitting
- Must pass the 3 C's: Clarity, Context, Curiosity (see hook-formulas KB)
- Must raise a question the viewer NEEDS answered
- Must be 100% deliverable — never overpromise
- Write 3 variations — the owner selects the strongest
- Max 10 words per sentence

#### Pre-Hook Note (include in every script)
```
PRE-HOOK NOTE: If an authentic, unscripted reaction moment was captured during filming —
place it here before the spoken hook begins. The Gmail Dot Scam pre-hook moment is the
clearest example of why this matters (75% 3-second retention).
```

#### Why the Word Count Limit Exists
One extra word in the hook = one extra fraction of a second the viewer decides to scroll. At 228,000+ views, a 1% improvement in 3-second retention is worth ~2,000 additional viewers. Every word past 15 costs views.

#### Failure Modes
```
❌ "Hey guys, today I want to talk about a really scary new scam that's going around."
   WHY IT FAILS: Greeting + no knowledge gap + vague ("a scam")

❌ "THIS one thing on your phone is giving scammers access."
   WHY IT FAILS: "THIS one thing" has zero Clarity. What thing? What access?

❌ "Imagine your mum lists a couch for sale online."
   WHY IT FAILS: Excludes everyone whose mum doesn't sell things online. Audience too narrow.
```

#### Good Hooks
```
✅ "Scammers are now claiming that they have photos of you in order to gain control of your WhatsApp account."
   WHY IT WORKS: Clarity (WhatsApp scam), Context (photos of you), Curiosity (how??)

✅ "Scammers have found a way to open fake PayPal accounts using your Gmail address — without your password."
   WHY IT WORKS: Specific app + shocking mechanism + knowledge gap opened
```

### PART 2 — CONTEXT
**Duration:** ~10 seconds spoken | **Word count:** 2–4 sentences

#### What It Does
Places the viewer inside an ordinary moment — before the scam hits. This is the Story Entry. The viewer recognises themselves in the normal situation, which makes the scam feel personal when it arrives.

#### Rules
- Start with what is NORMAL — before anything goes wrong
- Assume the viewer knows NOTHING about this scam
- Zero tech jargon — if a 70-year-old can't understand it, rewrite it
- Second person ("you") where possible — keep the viewer inside the scene
- Max 10 words per sentence

#### Story Entry Patterns
Use one of these to open the Context section:

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| Place the viewer in a routine moment | `"You're checking your emails. Nothing unusual."` |
| Introduce a normal-seeming disruption | `"One day your phone randomly loses all signal. Zero bars."` |
| Name a specific real-world scenario | `"Let's say your email is peterpan@gmail.com."` |

Immediately after the story entry, introduce the **Scene Beat** — the moment the ordinary becomes suspicious. One sentence. Specific.

```
Story entry: "You're checking your emails. Nothing unusual."
Scene beat:  "Then you notice the sender's address is yours."
```

#### Failure Modes
```
❌ "Many people use Gmail every day."
   WHY IT FAILS: Information, not story. The viewer is not inside anything.

❌ "SIM swapping is a growing threat in today's digital landscape."
   WHY IT FAILS: Jargon + generic + no scene. The viewer is not placed in a moment.

❌ "Online scams are becoming more sophisticated and harder to detect."
   WHY IT FAILS: No specific scenario. No viewer placement. No personal relevance.
```

#### Good Context
```
✅ "This WhatsApp scam is rising so fast because it comes directly from one of your contacts
   in an existing conversation — not from a random number."
   WHY IT WORKS: Grounded in something familiar. Visual anchor (existing conversation).
   Explains WHY this scam is different.
```

### PART 3 — TENSION
**Duration:** ~15 seconds spoken | **Word count:** 2–4 sentences

#### What It Does
Raises the stakes. Moves the scam from abstract to personal. The viewer must feel the threat is real, imminent, and happening to someone like them. This is a **scene** — not a list.

#### Rules
- Write a scene — second person, present tense, specific moment
- Connect the scam to something the viewer cares about (bank, family, privacy)
- Escalate the stakes: personal threat first → family threat second
- Never be preachy or condescending — no "if you're a good person" language
- Never split into multiple tension sections — there is exactly ONE tension section
- Never write a list of threats — write one scene with escalating stakes
- Max 10 words per sentence

#### Before Writing Tension — Name the Emotion
Choose one target emotion before writing:

| Emotion | Trigger Example |
|---|---|
| Fear of financial loss | "They can drain your account in minutes" |
| Fear of identity theft | "They're already using your name" |
| Fear of family harm | "Your parents would have no idea" |
| Shame of being fooled | "You already confirmed it without thinking" |
| Urgency of imminent threat | "It's happening right now" |

Write the scene that triggers that emotion. One specific moment. Not a list of consequences.

#### Approved Tension Patterns

**The "While You..." Pattern** — places the viewer in the innocent moment while revealing what's happening behind the scenes:
```
✅ "While you sit there wondering why your signal is dead — they already have your number."
✅ "You're clueless. Until one day you get an email from PayPal."
```

**The "You Can't..." Pattern** — stack things the viewer can no longer do, building to the real consequence:
```
✅ "You can't make calls. You can't load Instagram. But the real problem is your phone number."
```

**The "Connected To" Pattern** — link the scam to something the viewer cares about deeply:
```
✅ "It's connected to your 2-factor authentication on every account. Your bank sends security codes by text."
```

**The Escalation Pattern** — personal threat first, family threat last:
```
✅ "They can read all your messages, photos and documents — your banking information.
   And then they use your account to send the same scam to your contacts.
   Especially the most vulnerable ones. Like your parents. Like your grandparents."
```

#### Failure Modes
```
❌ "They want your personal information. They create urgency. They want your bank details."
   WHY IT FAILS: Third person. List of actions. No scene. No personal threat.

❌ "Scammers use this technique to steal your identity and access your accounts."
   WHY IT FAILS: Generic. No specificity. The viewer is not inside the danger.

❌ "If you're a good person who cares about security, you would never fall for this."
   WHY IT FAILS: Preachy. Condescending. Excludes large portions of the audience.

❌ Writing two tension sections
   WHY IT FAILS: There is ONE tension section. Splitting it breaks the structure and
   creates a double-revelation that dilutes the pivot.
```

---

### PART 4 — PIVOT
**Duration:** ~15 seconds spoken | **Word count:** 2–4 sentences

#### What It Does
Reveals HOW the scam works — the mechanism. This is the "aha" moment. After the tension placed the viewer inside the danger, the pivot explains the trick. The viewer now understands what was happening.

#### Rules
- Reveal the scam mechanism clearly — step by step if needed
- One explanation — no technical deep-dives
- Use definitive language ONLY — never "most likely", "might be", "could potentially"
- If it is a scam, say it is. No hedging.
- Use Visual Anchors — describe what the viewer would SEE (a page, a notification, a code)
- Max 10 words per sentence

#### What Is a Visual Anchor?
A Visual Anchor is a specific, concrete visual element the viewer can picture:

| Weak (abstract) | Strong (visual anchor) |
|---|---|
| "A fake website appears" | "A pixel-perfect login page for Facebook" |
| "You get notifications" | "Two notifications appear on your phone" |
| "They send a code" | "An eight-digit code appears on the fake site" |
| "They gain access" | "BOOM. Scammers have full access to your account." |

Visual Anchors increase watch time because the viewer is mentally constructing the scene. Every pivot should have at least 2.

#### Failure Modes
```
❌ "The scammer most likely uses your information to access your accounts."
   WHY IT FAILS: "Most likely" = hedging. If it's a scam, say it is.

❌ "They employ sophisticated social engineering techniques and credential harvesting
   via pixel-perfect phishing pages."
   WHY IT FAILS: Jargon. "Social engineering", "credential harvesting" mean nothing
   to the target audience.

❌ A pivot that runs to 6+ sentences
   WHY IT FAILS: The pivot explains the mechanism — it does not cover all variations,
   all scenarios, and all edge cases. One mechanism. Clearly explained.
```

#### Good Pivot
```
✅ "If you click on that link, it brings you to a practically pixel-perfect login page
   for Facebook. After typing in your phone number, you get two notifications.
   The first is WhatsApp asking if you want to link another device.
   The second is on the fake site — an eight-digit code.
   Once you enter that code into WhatsApp, scammers have full access to your account."

   WHY IT WORKS: Step-by-step. Multiple Visual Anchors. Definitive language.
   Viewer would recognise this if it happened to them.
```

### PART 5 — PAYOFF
**Duration:** ~15 seconds spoken | **Word count:** 2–4 sentences

#### What It Does
Closes the curiosity loop. Delivers the actionable advice the viewer watched for. Rewards them for staying to the end. Frames sharing as a moral act — protect your family.

#### Rules
- Maximum 3 tips — never 4 or more (viewer overwhelm kills retention at the end)
- At least 1 tip the viewer can action TODAY, right now on their phone
- Each tip gets 2 sentences: the ACTION and the REASON
- Stay in friend voice — NOT institutional/leaflet language
- End with forward motion: "share this", "check your settings", "do this NOW"
- Never end with a product pitch or follow request inside the script
- Max 10 words per sentence

#### Friend Voice vs Leaflet Voice
Every tip has two versions. Always use the friend voice.

| Leaflet Voice ❌ | Friend Voice ✅ |
|---|---|
| "Enable two-factor authentication for better security." | "Turn on two-step login in your Gmail settings. It means even if they have your password, they still can't get in." |
| "Verify the legitimacy of emails before taking any action." | "Never confirm an email you didn't request." |
| "Monitor your financial accounts for suspicious activity." | "Go to PayPal and check if any accounts use a dotted version of your email. If yes — report it immediately." |
| "Contact your telecommunications provider to set up a PIN." | "Call your mobile provider today. Set a Transfer Pin or Port-Out Lock." |
| "Be cautious when sharing personal information online." | "If someone asks for your bank details over text — stop. Your bank will never do that." |

#### The Sharing CTA
End every script with the OHN sharing call to action. Adapt the wording to the scam — never copy verbatim.

**Template:**
```
"Sadly, this scam is mostly targeting [vulnerable_group].
So [share this / send this to / show this to] your [parents / grandparents / family]
to keep them safe."
```

**Why it works:** Frames sharing as a protective act. The viewer is not being asked to like and subscribe — they are being asked to protect someone they love. This is the core of OHN's viral mechanism.

#### Failure Modes
```
❌ Payoff with 4 tips
   WHY IT FAILS: Viewer overwhelm. After 3 tips the audience stops retaining.
   A 4-tip payoff causes drop-off right before the CTA.

❌ "Stay safe online, everyone."
   WHY IT FAILS: Sign-off. Generic. Not actionable. The script ends after the payoff action,
   not after a sign-off.

❌ "Check out our other videos for more tips on staying safe."
   WHY IT FAILS: Product pitch inside the script. Never allowed.
```

#### Good Payoff
```
✅ "Here's how to check if you have any ghost-paired devices on your account.
   Go to Settings in WhatsApp. Then to Link Devices.
   If you don't recognise any device, click on it and log it out.
   Sadly, this scam targets older generations — so share this with your parents and grandparents."

   WHY IT WORKS: Specific app + specific setting + specific action. Viewer can do this
   in 30 seconds. Ghost-paired devices is memorable and visual. CTA frames sharing
   as protecting family.
```

## Voice Breaks — Transition Phrases

Every OHN script uses a **Voice Break** between sections: a short phrase (max 5 words) that signals a gear change. Use these only. Do not invent new ones.

| Voice Break | Position | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| `"Here's the thing."` | Context → Tension | Signals the hidden danger is coming. The viewer leans in. |
| `"Here's how it works."` | Tension → Pivot | Signals the mechanism reveal. Brain resets — paying attention again. |
| `"Here's what to do."` | Pivot → Payoff | Signals actionable tips. Viewer switches from passive to active. |

#### Bad Voice Breaks
```
❌ "But what you don't know is..."  — Too long, too dramatic
❌ "So what can you do about it?"  — Too generic, not OHN voice
❌ "Let me tell you something important."  — Too formal, sounds like a lecture
```

## Hard Rules — Non-Negotiable

Every script must satisfy ALL of these before submission:

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| Total word count: 130–150 words | Fits within 60 seconds spoken. Over 150 = over-length video = algorithm penalty. |
| Every sentence: max 10 words | Shorter sentences = easier to read aloud = fewer stumbles = more natural delivery. |
| Zero tech jargon | Target audience: adults 35–65. "Multi-factor authentication via TOTP" loses 80% of the audience. |
| Zero filler words | Every word must earn its place. "Really", "very", "basically", "just" all waste runtime. |
| Write for women aged 35–44 in USA/Canada/UK/Australia | This is the core OHN audience persona. If it doesn't feel relevant to them — rewrite. |
| Never use "scammer" more than twice | Vary with "they", "these people", "criminals". Repetition sounds robotic. |
| Never add a Closing or Sign-off section | The script ends after the Payoff CTA. "Stay safe out there" is banned. |
| No product pitch or follow request inside the script | Never. Not even at the end of the payoff. |
| One Tension section only | No second tension section. No "Tension 2". One escalating scene. |
| Maximum 3 Payoff tips | Never 4. Ever. |
| 3 Hook variations submitted | Owner selects. Scripty writes all 3. |

## Banned Words and Phrases

| Banned | Use Instead |
|---|---|
| "Senior" or "elderly" | "parents", "grandparents", "older adults" |
| "Hacker" (dramatic) | "scammer" (max 2× per script) |
| "Cyber" anything | Rephrase in plain language |
| "Hey guys" / "Welcome back" | Nothing — start the script instantly |
| "Stay safe out there" | End at the CTA — no sign-off |
| "Guarantee" | Never promise outcomes |
| "Most likely" / "might be" / "could potentially" | Definitive language only in the Pivot |
